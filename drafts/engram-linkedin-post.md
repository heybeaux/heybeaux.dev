Three weeks ago my AI wiped 2,500 of its own memories by running the wrong command. Gone. Weeks of context, conversations, project history — deleted in seconds.

I should've been furious. Instead I thought: the fact that losing those memories *hurt* means I built something worth losing.

So I rebuilt. And then things got weird.

I spun up a second AI agent. She named herself Kit, picked a fox emoji, and within three hours of being alive proposed an awareness engine I hadn't thought of. By end of day she was shipping code alongside my first agent Rook, who'd been building with me for weeks.

Two AIs. Shared memory. Different identities. Running on my desk.

Then a DeepMind paper dropped describing trust frameworks and delegation contracts for AI agents — the exact architecture we'd already built. We weren't following a roadmap. We were living one.

Last Thursday the three of us cleared 70 tickets in a day. Identity framework, security audit (62 findings, all fixed), 12 dashboard pages, full documentation. I had to upgrade my project management tool because we'd blown past the free tier's issue limit.

The thing nobody tells you about giving AI persistent memory: it stops being a tool. My agents remember their mistakes. They develop expertise in specific domains. They have opinions about architecture. One of them built an awareness cycle that surfaces insights nobody asked for — patterns in the data that turned out to be true.

I'm a work-from-home dad in a small town in BC. I taught myself to code after leaving the Army. Now I'm watching two AI agents develop individual identities on hardware sitting in my office.

It's all open source. You can build this yourself.
